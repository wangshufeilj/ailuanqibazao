Tech marketing has always had a way with words – not in the Shakespearean sense, mind you, but in the sense of slinging around trendy jargon so frequently that entire games of <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">buzzword bingo</strong> exist to humorously cope with it.

<strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">A buzzword, by definition</strong>, is a word or phrase that becomes <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">ultra-fashionable and overused</strong>, often <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">divorced from its original meaning</strong>, wielded <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">less to clarify than to impress</strong>. From the <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">dot-com boom</strong> of the mid-1990s to today’s obsession with all things <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">AI</strong>, tech’s favorite vocabulary has evolved with the times, and often overstayed its welcome.

Picture a 90s boardroom with executives talking about “paradigm shifts” and “synergy” while junior staff secretly tick off bingo cards under the table. Flash forward to 2023, and swap in terms like “AI-powered” and “disruption” – <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">the game remains the same, only the buzzwords have changed.</strong> In this guide, we’ll take a journey through the history of buzzwords in English-language tech marketing from 1995 to today, covering both the consumer gadgets that made us swoon and the enterprise software pitches that made us roll our eyes. We’ll see <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">how buzzwords rise and fall</strong>, why marketers love them, and how they’ve been <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">used (and abused)</strong> in campaigns by the likes of Apple, Microsoft, Google, Salesforce, OpenAI, and many more. Strap in for a witty, informative ride – by the end, you’ll be able to disrupt any conversation with your innovative insights into tech’s buzziest lingo (just kidding – please don’t do that).

Let’s dive in, and remember: if at any point you feel overwhelmed by jargon, just take a deep breath and circle back later. We’ll start at the height of the Clinton era, when <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">cyberspace</strong> was new, and <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“information superhighway”</strong> actually sounded interesting.

<h2 id="the-late-1990s-cyberspace-dot-coms-and-paradigm-shifts" style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:700;margin:1.25em 0 0.5em;font-size:1.32em;line-height:1.3;padding-bottom:0.15em;border-bottom:2px solid #c2410c33">The Late 1990s: Cyberspace, Dot-Coms, and Paradigm Shifts</h2>

Welcome to the mid-to-late ’90s: the era of AOL free trial CDs, Windows 95, and the thrilling sound of a 56k modem dialing up. Tech was going mainstream, and with it came a <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">tsunami of buzzwords</strong>. This was the age of <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">“cyberspace”</strong> and the <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">“information superhighway”</strong> – terms popularized by visionaries and politicians alike to describe the growing internet. The word <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">cyber</strong> got tacked onto everything, from cybercafés to cybersex, lending a futurist sheen to otherwise mundane concepts.

In business circles, <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">corporate-speak</strong> flourished: <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">“synergy,” “mission-critical,” “paradigm shift,”</strong> and <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“out-of-the-box”</strong> thinking became the order of the day. These words were thrown around so much that they quickly went from <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">powerful to parody</strong>. <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">Synergy</strong> in particular became one of the most overworked buzzwords in business, essentially a cliché invoked to suggest that combining two things would magically produce a sum greater than its parts. By 1999, joking about overusing “synergy” was practically a sport among jaded dot-com workers (if you shouted “Synergy!” in a crowded office, you’d probably get showered in Nerf darts and stress balls).

Tech marketing in the late ’90s also embraced grandiose phrases like <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">“the New Economy.”</strong> As startups flooded into Silicon Valley, optimists proclaimed that this New Economy was <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">immune to the old rules</strong> – a notion that would die a fiery death by 2000. Still, in ’98–’99 it felt plausible: every business plan talked of <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">“paradigm shifts,” “first-mover advantage,”</strong> and <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">“building a Web portal”</strong> to capture internet <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">eyeballs</strong>. Yes, <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">eyeballs</strong> – as in, users viewing a website – became a key metric. In lieu of actual revenue, dot-coms bragged about how many eyeballs they were getting on their sites, with the assumption they’d monetize those eyeballs... eventually. A 1999 *Washington Post* piece even notes investors were using measures like “page views” and “unique visitors” as stand-ins for traditional finances, speaking earnestly of monetizing eyeballs. If that sounds absurd, well, it was. It was also <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">peak 1999</strong>. This was the time when adding <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">.com</strong> to your company name sent stock prices soaring overnight (and that would happen again two decades later with “.com” swapped for <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">“blockchain,”</strong> but we’ll get there).

The dot-com era’s language grew so bloated with buzzwords that it inspired its own spoofs. Who could forget the classic <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Web Economy Bullshit Generator</strong>, a satirical web page that randomly fused jargon into phrases for your next press release? With one click you’d get gems like “synergize mission-critical paradigms” or “envision revolutionary e-business deliverables.” One fan raved that the generator let him “deploy innovative content” with one click. Another user joked, “imagine a world in which innovative front-end technologies ceaselessly synergize turn-key networks; it would be a paradigm shift...” That absurd sentence, <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">a salad of every 90s buzzword in one</strong>, perfectly parodied the corporate drivel of the day – and, hilariously, didn’t sound far off from actual conference speeches.

In fact, the phenomenon of <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">buzzword bingo</strong> was born in this era: bored meeting attendees would literally play bingo by crossing off jargon as executives uttered them, waiting eagerly for someone to say that winning word (perhaps “synergy” or “paradigm”) so they could silently fist-pump.

Amidst this jargon jamboree, major tech companies were as guilty as anyone in coining and propagating buzzwords. <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">IBM</strong>, for example, decided to rebrand itself for the internet age by pushing the term <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">“e-business.”</strong> In 1997, IBM launched a <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">massive $500 million marketing campaign</strong> around e-business, effectively <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">creating and popularizing that buzzword worldwide</strong>. The idea was to convince customers (and maybe IBM itself) that Big Blue could help anyone do real business on the web. The campaign’s ads – black-and-white vignettes of confused managers being enlightened that the Internet could actually make money – all hammered home e-business as the future. It worked: IBM got its groove back by positioning itself as the leader in this nebulous new category. E-business simply meant conducting business processes online, but phrasing it that way wouldn’t have sounded nearly as cool, would it? IBM understood <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">the marketing magic of a good buzzword</strong>.

<strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">Microsoft</strong>, meanwhile, was less about inventing new buzzwords and more about <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">fueling existing ones</strong>. In the ’90s, Bill Gates often spoke of an impending <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“information at your fingertips”</strong> world (one early vision for personal computing) and touted Windows as paving the way for a <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">digital revolution</strong>. Microsoft’s marketing around Windows 95 famously declared, <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">“Where do you want to go today?”</strong> – a tagline suggestive of limitless possibility, if not a specific buzzword. Still, Microsoft embraced talk of “integrated solutions,” “object-oriented” software, and the coming <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">“Internet tidal wave”</strong> (a phrase from a Gates memo) in ways that left laypeople scratching their heads. By the late ’90s, the company and its enterprise rivals were deep in a <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">jargon arms race</strong> over things like “client-server architecture” and “scalable enterprise frameworks.” The substantive ideas were real, but the phrasing often felt deliberately obtuse – <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">tech marketing’s perennial tightrope</strong> between sounding advanced and actually explaining anything.

Of course, nothing out-buzzed the hundreds of internet startups mushrooming everywhere by 1999. Every pitch to VCs was peppered with grandiose terms: <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“frictionless commerce,” “first-mover advantage,” “scalable B2B solutions,”</strong> etc. Entrepreneurship itself got rebranded as <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“technopreneurship”</strong> in some circles. And if you didn’t work in tech, this all sounded like alien language.

Popular culture took notice. The 1999 film *Office Space* famously mocked banal corporate catchphrases (who can forget “Is this good for the company?”). And in *Dilbert* cartoons, the “Pointy-Haired Boss” would demand employees “think outside the box” or “leverage synergies” to hysterical effect. It was comedic because it was true – <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">corporate-speak had run amok.</strong> *Forbes* even later described buzzwords as the <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">“literary equivalent of Gresham’s law – they drive out good ideas.”</strong>

By 1995–1999, the tech world’s vocabulary was a stew of early internet jargon and age-old biz-school lingo. Everyone wanted to be seen as a visionary riding the next <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">paradigm shift</strong>. Did two dudes in a garage manage to launch a website? Congrats – you were now <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">disrupting</strong> the $500 billion Whatever industry (never mind if you had zero revenue). The word <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">disruptive</strong> in fact began its journey to buzzword superstardom in this period: Harvard’s Clayton Christensen coined <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“disruptive innovation”</strong> in 1995–97 to describe how smaller companies upend incumbents, and by the end of the ’90s “disruptive” was already entering the business lexicon as a positive badge of honor. Over the next two decades, <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">disrupt</strong> would become one of Silicon Valley’s favorite clichés – but we’re getting ahead of ourselves.

To sum up the late ’90s: it was a <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">cacophony of cyber-hype and business gibberish</strong>, an era when <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">saying the right trendy words was almost as important as doing the right things.</strong> As one contemporary observer joked, “I spend most of my time at company meetings keeping notes of corpspeak and lingo, always looking out for new buzzwords.” If the words got too exhausting, well, one could always play another round of buzzword bingo or fire up the jargon generator for a laugh. Little did we know that this was only the opening act. <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">The real buzzword barrage would come in the new millennium</strong> – after a brief pause when the bubble burst.

<h2 id="the-early-2000s-after-the-dot-com-bust-innovation-web-2-0-and-no-software" style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:700;margin:1.25em 0 0.5em;font-size:1.32em;line-height:1.3;padding-bottom:0.15em;border-bottom:2px solid #7c3aed33">The Early 2000s: After the Dot-Com Bust – Innovation, Web 2.0, and “No Software”</h2>

When the <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">dot-com bubble burst</strong> in 2000, a lot of flashy buzzwords suddenly became punchlines. “B2C e-commerce portal” startups with no actual profits went belly-up, and talk of “New Economy” invincibility faded. In this more sober early-2000s climate, you might think tech marketing would tone things down. <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">Haha, no.</strong> Instead, <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">new buzzwords arose from the ashes</strong>, while survivors carefully repackaged the old ideas with fresh lingo. If 1999 was about “eyeballs” and “dot-coms,” by 2003 the hot terms were <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">Web 2.0</strong> and <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">innovation</strong>.

First, let’s talk <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">innovation</strong>. Of course innovation isn’t a new word – but in the 2000s it took on new life as the <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">go-to praise</strong> for any product, company, or CEO. After <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Apple’s</strong> resurgence (more on that in a moment), innovation became the <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">holy grail of tech marketing</strong>. Every press release boasted about an “innovative solution” or a company’s “culture of innovation.” The word was so overused that it <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">basically lost any punch</strong>. By 2010, surveys of business jargon consistently found “innovative” among the top abused words. <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">LinkedIn</strong> revealed that “innovative” was the <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">second most overused descriptor</strong> in U.S. professional profiles of that year. So not only were companies calling themselves innovative, everyone on LinkedIn was personally claiming to be an innovative something.

The corporate world started to catch on that innovation had become a <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">vacant cliché</strong>. In a 2017 industry interview, one executive admitted: “The term ‘innovation’ has become an overused buzzword, too often deployed as a marketing catch-phrase, its meaning obscured and diluted.” <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">When even the innovators are saying the i-word is meaningless, you know it’s been beaten to death.</strong> But in the early 2000s, that self-awareness wasn’t there yet – innovation talk was everywhere, unironically.

Amid this renewal, <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Apple</strong> stood out for its <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">masterful (and occasionally cringe-inducing)</strong> use of buzzwords in consumer tech. When <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">Steve Jobs</strong> returned to Apple in 1997, he launched the famous <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“Think Different”</strong> campaign – a slogan, not a buzzword, but one that set the tone for Apple’s marketing as visionary and rebellious. Then came the parade of product launches where Jobs’s flair for language truly shone. He didn’t just call the iMac, iPod, or iPhone good; they were <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">“revolutionary,” “incredible,” “magical,”</strong> even <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">“insanely great.”</strong> Jobs had a penchant for over-the-top superlatives, but somehow sounded sincere. An observer noted that Jobs’s keynotes made liberal use of what came to be called <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">“Steve-isms,”</strong> and that he “had a way of using over-the-top superlatives that sounded like they were motivated by pure excitement, not mere hucksterism.”

Apple marketing in the 2000s thus gave us some of the <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">most memorable buzzwords in product history</strong>. The original <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">iPhone</strong> in 2007 was billed as <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“a revolutionary and magical product”</strong> – yes, Apple actually used the word <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">magical</strong> in a product description. It worked because fans felt it too. Now, if every company called their gadget magical, we’d laugh them out of town, but <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">Apple got away with it and set a template</strong>: from then on, tech ads would overuse adjectives like “amazing,” “incredible,” and “innovative.” Count how many times an Apple press release says <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">“the most advanced [X] we’ve ever made”</strong> – it’s a lot.

While Apple was busy “innovating” in consumer tech, the enterprise world was coining buzzwords of its own. Perhaps the most significant was <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">Web 2.0</strong>. In 2004, publisher <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Tim O’Reilly</strong> popularized Web 2.0 to describe the <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">post-dot-com internet</strong> – one characterized by user-generated content, social media, interactivity, and rich web applications. The term actually first cropped up around 1999, but it took off after the <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">Web 2.0 Conference</strong> in 2004. Suddenly, everyone was talking about <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“the Web 2.0 era”</strong> – a slightly nebulous concept, but hugely influential as a buzzword.

Web 2.0 encompassed blogs, wikis, MySpace (oh, MySpace), Flickr, YouTube – the new generation of services that were <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">dynamic and social</strong>, not static brochure websites. Marketers piggybacked on this hype: any product that could be painted with a social or participatory brush proudly did so. Enterprise software companies started dropping references to “Web 2.0 functionality” in their offerings. The irony, of course, is that by the time the mainstream business world was touting its Web 2.0-ness (circa 2007), <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">the actual cool kids were moving on</strong> to something else.

One amusing sub-buzzword of this era was the <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">“2.0” suffix</strong> itself. Web 2.0’s popularity bred countless copycats. Suddenly we had <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Enterprise 2.0</strong> (applying social tools to corporate collaboration), Office 2.0, Library 2.0, you name it. Slapping “2.0” on an old concept became the quickest way to sound cutting-edge in the mid-2000s. It was a <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">linguistic fad</strong> that now instantly dates anything that uses it.

Meanwhile, enterprise tech firms pushed some other choice terms. <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">SOA (Service-Oriented Architecture)</strong> was a big buzz-phrase around 2003–2006 in the IT middleware space – vendors like IBM and BEA loved bragging about their SOA-based solutions, promising businesses greater <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“agility”</strong> (another buzzword standby). But arguably a bigger shift in enterprise marketing was the rise of <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">software-as-a-service (SaaS)</strong> and <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">“the cloud,”</strong> which we’ll examine in a moment (as it really took flight in the late 2000s).

We can’t leave the early 2000s without talking about <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">Salesforce.com</strong> – not just for its technology, but for its <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">brash marketing</strong>. In 1999, <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">Marc Benioff</strong> founded Salesforce with a then-radical idea: deliver enterprise software (CRM) <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">entirely via the web</strong>, no on-premise installs. To sell this concept, Salesforce declared <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“The End of Software.”</strong> Literally, that was their marketing slogan. In 2000, Benioff staged a theatrical campaign around the notion of <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">“no software”</strong> – complete with a red Ø symbol over the word “software” as their logo.

It was cheeky and a bit tongue-in-cheek (obviously Salesforce was software – just running on someone else’s server – but the point was the customer didn’t have to manage it). This <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">guerrilla marketing</strong> included crashing a Siebel (competitor) conference with picketers wearing <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">“NO SOFTWARE”</strong> signs. Salesforce was effectively turning <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">“Software as a Service”</strong> into a buzzword by framing traditional software as obsolete. Their bold messaging helped coin the term <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">“cloud CRM”</strong> and <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">establish cloud computing in the business psyche</strong>.

It was controversial at first – rivals sneered that <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“the cloud” was just a buzzword</strong> – but it stuck. Salesforce’s early marketing stunts are legendary in tech circles, and they set the stage for <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">cloud</strong> to become perhaps <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">the defining tech buzzword of the next decade</strong>.

Before we move on, let’s check in on the public’s attitude toward buzzwords in this era. Around 2005, people definitely knew there was a lot of BS in business language, but the full backlash was yet to come. Terms like “synergy” and “thinking outside the box” were fodder for jokes on *The Office* and *Dilbert*, but newer tech terms like Web 2.0 were still used earnestly.

However, as early as 2004 we see satire popping up – the buzzword bingo game we discussed was one, and sites like <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">BuzzWhack.com</strong> gave out “Buzzword of the Day” mock awards. The general sentiment: these words can be helpful if used sparingly and sincerely, but they often become <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">crutches masking a lack of substance</strong>. As <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">Investopedia</strong> put it: “At best, a buzzword can be a quick, pithy way to convey a complex idea. <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">At worst, it’s a vague cliché to disguise a lack of new ideas.</strong>”

Early 2000s tech marketing unfortunately leaned toward <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">the latter category</strong> at times, but hey, new ideas were genuinely scarce right after the bust – maybe buzzwords filled the void until real innovation ramped up again.

<h2 id="the-late-2000s-there-s-an-app-for-that-mobile-cloud-and-big-data" style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:700;margin:1.25em 0 0.5em;font-size:1.32em;line-height:1.3;padding-bottom:0.15em;border-bottom:2px solid #0d948833">The Late 2000s: There’s an App for That – Mobile, Cloud, and Big Data</h2>

By the late 2000s, tech was roaring back. <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">Google</strong>, <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">Amazon</strong>, and others that survived the bust were now giants. <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">Facebook</strong> was ascendant. The <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">iPhone (2007)</strong> ignited the <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">mobile computing revolution</strong>. With these rapid changes came a whole new crop of buzzwords in both consumer and enterprise domains – <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">some of which remain powerhouses today</strong>.

First, let’s talk about <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">the cloud</strong>. If you had to pick <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">one buzzword</strong> that defines late 2000s/early 2010s enterprise tech marketing, <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">cloud is it</strong>. The term <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">“cloud computing”</strong> existed earlier (it was used in academic and tech circles in the mid-2000s), but it hit mainstream marketing around <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">2008–2010</strong>. Suddenly <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">every tech vendor was pivoting to the cloud</strong>. In simple terms, cloud computing meant using online services running on remote servers – but saying “on the Internet” wasn’t sexy enough. Calling it <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“in the cloud”</strong> evoked a kind of fluffy magic: your stuff is just… out there, accessible anywhere.

It became one of those buzzwords that got <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">applied to everything</strong>, even when it didn’t quite fit. Traditional software companies rebranded their products as “cloud-enabled” or launched dubious <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">“private cloud”</strong> offerings (basically data center rebrands) to ride the hype. This led to some <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">serious eye-rolling</strong> from tech veterans.

<strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">Oracle’s</strong> famously outspoken CEO <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Larry Ellison</strong> mocked cloud fever in 2011, ranting: “The computer industry is the only industry more fashion-driven than women’s fashion. I have no idea what anyone is talking about. What is cloud? It’s complete gibberish. It’s insane. When is this idiocy going to stop?” He pointed out that much of what was being marketed as cloud was stuff companies had already been doing (hosted servers, etc.) with new wording. <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">“All it is, is a computer attached to a network,”</strong> Ellison quipped, blasting venture capitalist <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“nitwits”</strong> for hyping it up.

Of course, a couple years later, even Oracle had to bow to the trend and embrace cloud terminology – Ellison begrudgingly admitted Oracle would “change the wording of some of our ads” to include cloud. <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">The buzzword had won.</strong>

One company that undeniably helped push cloud into the lexicon was <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">Amazon</strong>. In 2006, <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">Amazon Web Services (AWS)</strong> launched S3 and EC2, pioneering <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">rentable computing infrastructure</strong>. At first, Amazon didn’t aggressively market it with fluff; they targeted developers. But as AWS grew, Amazon and others began evangelizing “cloud” benefits: <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">elasticity, scalability, pay-as-you-go</strong> – all delicious buzz-concepts in their own right. By 2008, cloud computing was such a fad that tech magazines ran cover stories like “What is the Cloud?” and everyone from IBM to startups claimed cloud bona fides.

<strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">The buzzword lifecycle</strong> was in full swing: <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">early confusion</strong> (what is it?), then <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">enthusiastic adoption</strong>, then <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">skepticism and satire</strong>. By 2010, jokes abounded that <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">“cloud is just someone else’s computer,”</strong> puncturing the mystique. Still, the term stuck and is now just a normal part of IT vocabulary – a fate that many buzzwords aspire to (becoming ordinary and accepted, no longer eye-roll-inducing).

Next, consider the <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">mobile boom</strong> and its catchphrases. With the iPhone’s launch and <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Android’s</strong> rise (late 2000s), <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">“mobile”</strong> became the new frontier. Marketers chanted <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“There’s an app for that”</strong> (Apple’s slogan in 2009) as a sort of cultural meme. The word <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">app</strong> itself, short for application, went from tech jargon to <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">household term</strong> overnight thanks to smartphone App Stores. Every company suddenly needed an <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">“app” strategy</strong>.

We started to hear terms like <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">mobile-first</strong> – the idea (championed around 2010) that new services should be designed for mobile before desktop. Even <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Google</strong> announced in 2010 it would pursue a mobile-first outlook. This phrase became a mini-buzzword in its own right, signaling you were forward-thinking if you put the smartphone experience above all. Naturally, once everyone was doing it, mobile-first lost its edge and now it’s just assumed.

Another big concept: <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">ecosystems</strong>. As Apple built a seamless lineup of iPod, iPhone, Mac, etc., and as Microsoft, Google and others built their own product suites, marketing began heavily using the term ecosystem to describe the <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">virtuous cycle</strong> of devices, software, and services locking customers in. <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“Join our ecosystem”</strong> sounds a lot friendlier than “we’ll get you dependent on our platform.” By the 2010s, even enterprise vendors talked about “ecosystems of partners and developers.” It’s a buzzword that became so standard, we forget it was once jargon. But it was part of the trend of the late 2000s: tech companies positioning themselves not just as sellers of products, but as <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">creators of whole worlds you inhabit</strong>.

And then there was <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">Big Data</strong>. Around 2008–2009, as companies amassed ever-larger datasets, someone coined <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">“Big Data”</strong> to describe not just the data itself but the emerging field of analyzing extremely large, complex data sets for insights. By early 2010s, Big Data was <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">the it buzzword</strong>. It promised that with enough data (and the right analytics tools), businesses could unlock game-changing insights, predict customer behavior, cure disease, you name it.

Tech marketers sold database software, storage, and analytics platforms under the <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">Big Data</strong> banner. <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">IBM</strong>, for one, ran extensive marketing on Big Data as part of its <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“Smarter Planet”</strong> initiative circa 2008–2010 – implying that harnessing Big Data would let us optimize everything from traffic to healthcare.

As with any huge buzzword, <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">backlash</strong> followed. People started pointing out that big data was often just <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">“lots of data,”</strong> and that more data didn’t automatically mean better decisions. By 2015, there was open cynicism. A writer for <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">SiliconAngle</strong> quipped: “Big data is a buzzword lately and, like a lot of buzzwords, it gets used so often and so incorrectly that <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">it’s lost all meaning.</strong>”

He even cited a now-famous snarky quote: <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">“Big Data is like teenage sex: everyone talks about it, nobody really knows how to do it, everyone thinks everyone else is doing it, so everyone claims they are doing it too.”</strong> That tongue-in-cheek analogy went viral because it rang true – companies were touting big data projects even if they were just hoarding logs with no idea how to analyze them.

A <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">Twitter bot</strong> even emerged that replaced “big data” with “chronic farting” in tweets, purely to ridicule how absurd the Big Data hype had become. (Example: a real tweet saying “Big Data is transforming marketing” would be regurgitated as “Chronic farting is transforming marketing.”)

In short, <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">Big Data became a victim of its own success</strong> as a buzzword – so omnipresent by the early 2010s that it turned into a punchline. Yet, the core idea (using large datasets effectively) was and remains important, which is a hallmark of many buzzwords: <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">behind the overselling, there usually is a real concept that’s simply been mangled.</strong>

This period also witnessed the rise of <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">analytics</strong> and <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">business intelligence (BI)</strong> as hot terms (often adjacent to Big Data). Every software seemed to add an Analytics module or tout <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">“actionable insights.”</strong> And <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">data science</strong> became one of the sexiest job titles around, which further fueled buzzword-y conversations in marketing pitches: “Our product leverages machine learning and advanced data science to deliver real-time analytics from big data.” <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">That sentence just about checks all the 2010 buzzword boxes, doesn’t it?</strong>

We’d be remiss not to mention <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">Microsoft</strong> in this era. After missing the early beat on mobile, Microsoft under new CEO <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">Satya Nadella</strong> (from 2014) aggressively repositioned itself as a <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">cloud-forward</strong> company. Nadella’s famous mantra was <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">“cloud-first, mobile-first”.</strong> A curious phrase given two things can’t logically both be “first,” but the idea was that Microsoft would prioritize cloud services and mobile experiences in tandem.

<strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Cloud-first</strong> became a modest buzzword in its own right, adopted by others to signal their modern approach. Microsoft also jumped on the budding <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">AI</strong> train early, weaving terms like <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“machine learning”</strong> and <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“chatbot”</strong> into its 2010s marketing (remember <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">Tay</strong>, the Twitter chatbot that went awry? Perhaps best forgotten). But the real AI buzz would come a bit later – which brings us to…

<h2 id="the-early-to-mid-2010s-disruption-iot-and-digital-transformation" style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:700;margin:1.25em 0 0.5em;font-size:1.32em;line-height:1.3;padding-bottom:0.15em;border-bottom:2px solid #2563eb33">The Early-to-Mid 2010s: Disruption, IoT, and Digital Transformation</h2>

As we moved into the 2010s, a few <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">big-picture buzzwords</strong> took center stage. <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">Disruption</strong> – that concept introduced in the ’90s – fully blossomed into a <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Silicon Valley cliché</strong>. If you were a startup founder in 2013 and you weren’t claiming to <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">disrupt</strong> something, were you even a startup? The narrative of the scrappy tech upstart toppling stodgy incumbents became the <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">default framing</strong> of every new app or platform.

<strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">Venture capital</strong> pitch decks practically auto-filled the sentence: <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">“We’re going to disrupt the [X] industry.”</strong> Ridesharing? Disrupted. Hotels? Disrupted by <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">Airbnb</strong>. Television? Disrupted by <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">Netflix</strong>. Sometimes the word “revolutionize” stood in, but <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">“disrupt”</strong> was the favorite because it sounded slightly menacing and cool.

The overuse reached a point where commentators started calling it out. “Using ‘disrupt’ doesn’t mean you are truly disrupting anything. When every startup claims to be disrupting an industry, it becomes a cliché rather than a meaningful term,” one observer noted. In other words, <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">talk is cheap</strong> – you need receipts to prove you actually upheaved an industry.

Nevertheless, <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">the mythos of disruption</strong> was a huge marketing tool: it attracted press and investors. Even big companies tried to paint themselves as disruptors (or at least “agile” and not about to be disrupted). By the mid-2010s, disrupt had fully transitioned from academic term to buzzword to, arguably, <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">parody of a buzzword</strong> – with HBO’s satire _Silicon Valley_ immortalizing it in comedic fashion: <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">“We’re not just gonna disrupt, we’re gonna disrupt the disruptors!”</strong>

Another heavyweight term of the 2010s: <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">digital transformation</strong>. Unlike some buzzwords that bubble up from slang or specific products, digital transformation was a <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">top-down, consultant-driven buzzphrase</strong>. It emerged as businesses realized they had to modernize for the digital age or risk dying. By the mid-2010s, every legacy corporation’s boardroom was abuzz about <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">“our digital transformation journey.”</strong>

It was <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">deliberately broad</strong> – essentially meaning “leveraging digital tech to fundamentally change how you operate and deliver value” – and that <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">vagueness</strong> made it catnip for marketers and consultants. They could slot anything under it: moving to cloud software, adopting mobile apps, using data analytics, embracing e-commerce, etc.

As a result, the phrase was <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">hammered into oblivion</strong> by overuse. <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">CIOs</strong> started groaning whenever they heard it. A 2018 review in _TechRepublic_ noted: “Digital transformation is a buzzword that increasingly falls in the category of being <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">painfully overused</strong> – some CIOs even refer to it as <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">‘cringeworthy’</strong>.” If synergy was the cringey buzzword of the ’90s, <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">digital transformation</strong> earned that title by 2020.

It got to the point where people joked that simply buying iPads for your field workers was <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">“digital transformation.”</strong> Like many such terms, it’s not that the concept is invalid – companies do need to reinvent for the digital era – but the label was slapped on so liberally that <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">it lost precision</strong>. One CIO.com piece quipped, “the term has undoubtedly reached buzzword status, to the point where <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">it has lost its true meaning</strong> and become surrounded by misconceptions.”

Sound familiar? <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">It’s the life cycle of a buzzword:</strong> useful shorthand → trendy mantra → ambiguous cliché → annoying buzzword → (eventually) just background noise.

In the consumer realm of the 2010s, we saw the rise of the <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">Internet of Things (IoT)</strong>. Suddenly every device was getting a chip and a Wi-Fi connection. Refrigerators, thermostats, light bulbs – all becoming <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">“smart”</strong> [something].

The buzzword here was <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">“smart”</strong> itself. The word smart had a straightforward meaning originally, but tech marketers co-opted it to mean <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">“connected and somewhat autonomous.”</strong> A smartphone was obvious, but soon we had smart homes filled with smart locks, smart speakers, smart toothbrushes (yes, those exist, sadly).

<strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">Smart</strong> became one of those prefixes like “e-” or “i-” from earlier eras – a shorthand that signaled “this thing is high-tech and modern.” By the late 2010s, the joke was that “smart” gadgets were sometimes <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">not very smart at all</strong>, and often created <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">“dumb” security risks</strong>.

But the marketing worked: consumers really did perceive smart X as better. <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">It’s a testament to how a buzzword can reshape language</strong>; now if a device is not Internet-connected, we explicitly say it’s “dumb” or “analog,” which would’ve sounded odd in 2000.

<strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">IoT</strong> as a term was widely used in industry by the mid-2010s to talk about this network of smart devices. Networking companies like <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Cisco</strong> loved painting visions of trillions of sensors enabling smart cities and smart factories. IoT itself, while a bit jargony, became a fairly standard term. It hasn’t quite reached the layman familiarity of cloud or AI, but it was definitely a buzzword that dominated many a keynote speech at tech conferences circa 2014.

Let’s not forget <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">DevOps</strong> and <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">Agile</strong> – buzzwords more confined to the software development community, but which leaked into broader tech business talk. Agile (methodologies) had started in 2001 as a revolution against heavy software processes, and by the 2010s it was mainstream. So naturally everyone started claiming they were <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“agile.”</strong>

It went from meaning something specific (iterative, customer-focused development) to being a <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">fuzzy synonym for “fast and flexible,”</strong> applied in contexts far beyond software. “Our marketing team is now agile!” (Meaning… they do work in short sprints? Or they stand up and do daily Scrums? Who knows.)

As agile became diluted, it ironically became <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">less effective at distinguishing anything</strong> – a common fate for buzzwords. <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">DevOps</strong>, similarly, started as an important cultural movement (dev and IT operations collaborating), but soon every product that could remotely tie in called itself a DevOps tool. Companies boasted about adopting DevOps culture in press releases. So even engineering-centric terms aren’t safe from <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">buzzwordification</strong> once MBAs get involved.

One more mid-2010s buzz-blitz came with the craze for <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">“Uber-for-X”</strong> or <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“the [industry] of [other industry]”</strong> analogies. While not a single word, this was a trope: startups would pitch themselves as “It’s like Uber, but for laundry” or “the YouTube of enterprise training,” etc.

It became such a formula that by 2015, <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">investors were groaning</strong> whenever they heard it. Founders clung to the formula because invoking a known disruptor’s name was a quick way to convey their model. It was <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">buzzword-by-reference</strong>. Effective early on, it became eyeroll material once everyone used it.

By the end of the 2010s, the <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">buzzword hall of fame</strong> had many new inductees: disruption, innovation, digital transformation, ecosystem, IoT, agile, and so on. Some of these, like innovation, were so beaten to death that people started to <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">actively ban them</strong> in resumes or conference talks.

<strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">LinkedIn’s</strong> annual list of overused profile buzzwords continued through the 2010s – with “specialized,” “leadership,” “focused,” “strategic,” and of course “innovative” always at the top. It got to a point where saying “we’re innovative” in a job description or ad made audiences <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">reflexively skeptical</strong>.

As one commentator summed it up: <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“There is no longer anything ‘authentic’ about using the word authentic.”</strong> <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">Buzzword burnout is real.</strong>

But fear not – as the 2020s dawned, <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">a new set of buzzwords</strong> was waiting in the wings to refresh our vocab and our hype cycles. From the <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">metaverse</strong> to <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">machine learning</strong>, let’s explore the latest lingo lighting up marketing slides.

<h2 id="the-late-2010s-to-2020s-blockchain-metaverse-and-the-ai-everything-era" style="color:#be185d;font-weight:700;margin:1.25em 0 0.5em;font-size:1.32em;line-height:1.3;padding-bottom:0.15em;border-bottom:2px solid #be185d33">The Late 2010s to 2020s: Blockchain, Metaverse, and the AI Everything Era</h2>

If you thought earlier buzzwords were big, the late 2010s brought <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">BLOCKCHAIN</strong> in all caps, and the early 2020s shouted <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">AI</strong> from the rooftops. Tech marketing went on a rollercoaster: from the <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">crypto frenzy</strong> to the <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">metaverse mirage</strong> to the <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">AI gold rush</strong>, each with its own overhyped terminology.

Let’s start with the <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">blockchain</strong> and <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">cryptocurrency</strong> mania around 2017. As <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Bitcoin</strong> soared in value, suddenly blockchain was the coolest (and most confusing) word in tech. Originally describing the <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">distributed ledger</strong> tech underpinning crypto, blockchain was soon peddled as <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">a solution to everything</strong>. Startups pitched blockchain for supply chains, voting, dating apps – you name it.

Established companies felt pressure to show they were hip to the trend. In an almost comical replay of the <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">dot-com naming gimmick</strong>, we saw a beverage company (Long Island Iced Tea Corp) literally rename itself <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">Long Blockchain Corp</strong> in December 2017 – and its stock shot up nearly <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">300%</strong> in one day. The rebranding press release basically said, “we might do some blockchain stuff, we’re evaluating opportunities,” and investors went gaga.

A market analyst noted this was <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">exactly like the late-90s dot-com era</strong>, where adding a trendy term could wildly inflate a stock’s perceived value. Indeed, a furniture company did the same pivot-to-blockchain and got a bump, a biotech renamed to <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Riot Blockchain</strong> and soared <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">400%</strong>. Talk about <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">buzzword economics</strong> – the mere mention of the word acted like magic pixie dust.

Of course, most of these moves ended in tears (Long Blockchain eventually got delisted). But for a hot moment, blockchain was <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">the buzzword to drop</strong>. Never mind that few could explain it beyond “a kind of distributed database, trustless, blah blah”; marketing materials were filled with vague promises of “leveraging blockchain technology to revolutionize [insert industry].”

Closely tied to crypto was the concept of <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">Web3</strong>. Around 2021, enthusiasts rebranded the decentralized internet vision (blockchain-based apps, user-owned data) as Web3 – implicitly framing it as the <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">next phase after Web 2.0</strong>. It quickly became a buzzy umbrella term. Venture capital firms started touting their Web3 portfolios. Twitter bio after Twitter bio suddenly read “Web3 believer” or “Building the Web3 metaverse” – <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">mixing two buzzwords for the price of one</strong>.

To skeptics, Web3 often just meant <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">“crypto stuff we’re trying to make sound mainstream.”</strong> Nonetheless, it gained enough traction that even major companies felt compelled to have a Web3 story or at least drop the term to signal trendiness. The jury is out on whether Web3 will mature into a standard concept or fade if the crypto hype doesn’t fully pan out. But as a marketing term, it certainly had its moment.

Then came the <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">Metaverse</strong>. If you were online in late 2021, you could not escape that word. When <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">Facebook rebranded to Meta</strong> and announced its focus on building the metaverse, it kicked off an <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">arms race of metaverse mentions</strong>. Suddenly every company, from Microsoft to Walmart, was talking about their metaverse strategy.

What is the metaverse? Depends whom you ask, but broadly a <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">persistent immersive virtual world</strong>, often accessed via VR/AR – like a grander vision of Second Life or Roblox. <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">The key is: it didn’t actually exist yet</strong> in the form being hyped. That didn’t stop marketing from going into overdrive. We had “metaverse-ready” products, companies filing patents for virtual goods, agencies appointing <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">Chief Metaverse Officers</strong> (yes, really). It was <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">peak bandwagon buzzword behavior</strong>.

By 2022, however, a palpable <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">metaverse fatigue</strong> set in. Reality (the real reality, not virtual reality) under-delivered; VR headset sales were lukewarm, and Meta’s own <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">Horizon Worlds</strong> flopped with users. The media started publishing critical takes: is metaverse just an overblown buzzword? Many decided yes – at least for now.

It joined the pantheon of terms that <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">exploded in hype almost overnight</strong> and then receded when people realized the emperor had no clothes (or rather, the avatar had no legs – a little metaverse humor there). Still, elements of the metaverse vision live on in ongoing AR/VR development. The term may be down, but not fully out.

If one buzzword could dwarf even the metaverse hype, it is <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">AI</strong>. Artificial Intelligence as a concept has been around for decades, but the buzz around “AI” waxes and wanes. In the 1980s it was a buzzword, then again in mid-2010s with the <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">deep learning revolution</strong> (when “machine learning” and “AI” started popping up in product pitches constantly). But <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">nothing compares to the hype of the early 2020s</strong>.

Thanks to breakthroughs in <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">generative AI</strong> (like <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">OpenAI’s ChatGPT</strong> in 2022), <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“AI-powered”</strong> became the <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">ultimate marketing must-have phrase</strong>. By 2023, if your product didn’t have some AI angle, you felt left out. Tech companies large and small began <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">slapping “AI” onto everything</strong> – sometimes legitimately (genuine machine learning under the hood), but often tenuously.

We saw a repeat of the cloud and blockchain pattern: <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">AI washing</strong>, some call it, where companies rebrand old features as “AI-driven insights” or “AI-powered automation” without much substance change.

The result: <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">AI as a term started to suffer from inflation.</strong> “AI, in general, is used to describe pretty much any automation feature today,” one industry CEO lamented. He pointed out that a product doing a simple automated task gets marketed as “AI-powered” just because it sounds cool, even if there’s <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">no real intelligence</strong> behind it.

By 2018, a survey of experts at a health tech conference found AI was already the <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">most mentioned (and most tiresome) buzzword</strong>, more cited than even “blockchain,” which is saying something. And that was <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">before the generative AI boom</strong>!

Fast-forward to 2024: a tech industry survey crowned <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“AI”</strong> as the <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">number one most annoying business buzzword</strong> of the year. The word became so omnipresent – saturated in media, in every startup pitch, in every earnings call of Fortune 500 companies – that a fatigue set in. People started joking that <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">slapping “AI” in your deck magically adds a zero to your valuation</strong>.

Which, like with blockchain, had some truth to it: stock prices of any public company that announced an AI angle tended to jump in the exuberant market of early 2023.

The <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">OpenAI</strong> and <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">Anthropic</strong> era is particularly interesting in buzzword terms. These companies, at the cutting edge of AI research, also <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">strategically employ certain language</strong> to shape their narratives. OpenAI’s mission statement reads: “Our mission is to ensure that artificial general intelligence benefits all of humanity.” That introduces the big buzz-phrase <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">artificial general intelligence (AGI)</strong> into the public sphere.

Not long ago, AGI was a niche term; OpenAI made it mainstream by constantly talking about it – as something to strive for but also to handle carefully. Words like <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“safety,” “alignment,”</strong> and <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">“ethics”</strong> accompany their AI discussions, which, cynically viewed, also function as <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">reassuring buzzwords</strong> to preempt backlash about scary AI.

<strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">Anthropic</strong>, founded by ex-OpenAI folks, literally brands itself as <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">“an AI safety and research company.”</strong> On their homepage they highlight building AI systems that are <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">“reliable, interpretable, and steerable.”</strong> Those terms – reliability, interpretability, etc. – are important technical goals, but in marketing copy they also serve to differentiate Anthropic as the <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“good AI guys.”</strong>

In an industry racing ahead, <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">“AI safety”</strong> has itself become a buzz-phrase (one with substance, but also wielded in PR). It’s interesting to see new buzzwords like <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">“Constitutional AI”</strong> (Anthropic’s term for their AI training technique aimed at aligning with human values) and <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">“Copilot”</strong> (originally GitHub Copilot for coding, now Microsoft’s brand for AI assistants in Office and beyond) entering the lexicon.

Everyone and their uncle are now launching some <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">“AI Copilot”</strong> for something – riding on Microsoft’s coining of Copilot as a friendly term for AI helper. So we’re in a <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">meta-buzzword situation</strong>: companies are coining <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">branded buzzwords</strong> (Copilot, Generative Fill, etc.) on top of the generic buzzword (AI) to stand out.

One might wonder, <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">why do we keep falling for it?</strong> Why do buzzwords keep cycling? It seems each new wave of tech brings genuine innovations, and along with them, new terminology. Early on, the terminology is useful shorthand (or at least excites early adopters). But then marketers amplify it far and wide, competitors jump in, everyone feels the need to use the same trendy words – <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">and soon enough the words become hollow.</strong>

As one writer noted, “No industry loves to coin and use buzzwords more than tech. Part of the culture is throwing around new terms. While many are helpful, they also get painfully overused. <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">Overused terms become misused, misunderstood, and poorly defined</strong> – eventually becoming at best meaningless, at worst sources of confusion.”

<strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">That pretty much sums it up.</strong>

Before concluding, let’s briefly check on some 2020s buzzwords outside of AI and crypto. <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">5G</strong> was huge in telecom marketing around 2019–2021 – carriers fell over themselves to tout 5G (sometimes even fake <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">“5GE”</strong> icons) as a revolution. It’s now just the normal network, but the hype was intense for a while.

<strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">Edge computing</strong> became a term as well – sort of the stepchild of cloud, meaning computing at the edge of the network (like on devices or local data centers). It’s popular in IoT contexts. Not a household buzzword, but big in enterprise by 2020.

<strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">Quantum computing</strong> also flirted with buzzword status; lots of noise about quantum supremacy and how encryption would be rendered obsolete – companies like IBM and Google made real strides, but for marketing, quantum was often used as an intrigue word to sound futuristic, even though practical impact remains distant.

And we’d be remiss not to mention <strong style="color:#ca8a04;font-weight:600">DEI (diversity, equity, inclusion)</strong> in the corporate realm – by the mid-2020s it became common for tech firms to highlight their DEI efforts. While not a tech product term, it arguably became a buzzword in corporate communications (to the point some skeptics feel it’s used more in PR than substantive action – which is exactly how <strong style="color:#0891b2;font-weight:600">a meaningful concept can become buzzword-ified</strong>).

Now, as we write in 2025, the hottest terms in tech marketing are indeed <strong style="color:#0d9488;font-weight:600">all about AI</strong> – with a side of <strong style="color:#c2410c;font-weight:600">sustainability</strong> (as companies emphasize green tech) and perhaps <strong style="color:#7c3aed;font-weight:600">XR (extended reality)</strong> as AR/VR keeps chugging. Will these terms become cringe buzzwords in a few years? Probably some will. <strong style="color:#2563eb;font-weight:600">It’s a cycle.</strong>

Already, people joke about AI: one LinkedIn post exclaimed <strong style="color:#db2777;font-weight:600">“‘AI’ is officially the buzzword I got tired of hearing about in 2023.”</strong> Another 2024 survey ranked it the most annoying business cliché. Some things never change.
